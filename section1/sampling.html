Introduction to Sampling

Principles of Representative Sampling

Theory

Bias

Bias occurs when a sample is selected in a way that favors certain characteristics, leading to overestimation or underestimation of the population parameter. Bias can be minimized or eliminated by using proper sampling techniques.

Types of Biased Sampling:

  • Convenience Sampling: Uses readily available data, risking severe bias. Results cannot be generalized to the population for inferential statistics but can be used for descriptive statistics of the sample.
  • Voluntary Response Sampling: A type of convenience sampling where participants volunteer, often over-representing those with strong opinions, making it unsuitable for population inferences.

Random Sampling

A random sample is selected by chance to ensure representativeness, allowing results to be extended to the population with calculable error probabilities. Random samples tend to mirror the populationâ€™s demographic and other characteristics.

Types of Random Sampling:

  • Simple Random Sample: Every possible sample of size \( n \) has an equal chance of being selected. This is the most common method used in this course.
  • Stratified Sampling: Divides the population into strata (e.g., by gender) and takes a random sample from each stratum to ensure representation of specific groups.
  • Systematic Sampling: Selects every \( k \)-th individual from a random starting point, effective only if the population is in random order.
  • Cluster Sampling: Divides the population into clusters (e.g., schools), randomly selects clusters, and samples all or some individuals within them.

Sampling Error

Sampling error arises because a statistic varies from sample to sample as a random variable. Key concepts include:

  • Sampling Variability: The extent to which a statistic varies across different samples.
  • Margin of Error: Quantifies the expected random sampling error, often expressed with 95% confidence. For proportions, it is calculated as \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \).
  • Random Sampling Error: Expected error from random sampling, covered by the margin of error.
  • Bad Sampling Methods: Non-random methods (e.g., convenience sampling) introduce bias, increasing error.
  • Sampling Frame: The list of all population individuals.
  • Under-coverage: When some population groups are excluded from the sampling frame, leading to bias.

Examples

Example 1: Identifying Bias

Determine whether each sampling method is likely to introduce bias and why:

  1. Restaurant comment cards are used to assess service quality.
  2. A new cancer treatment is tested on volunteers with severe cases.

1. Biased (Voluntary Response Sampling): Comment cards are typically filled out by customers with strong opinions (very satisfied or dissatisfied), not representing average experiences. Results cannot be generalized to all customers.

2. Biased (Voluntary Response Sampling): Volunteers with severe cancer cases are not representative of all patients, as they may differ in health status or treatment history, skewing results.

Example 2: Random Sampling

Suppose you want to estimate the average income of Kentucky residents. You select 1000 males from Lexington. Is this a simple random sample? Why or why not? How could you improve it?

Not a simple random sample. Selecting only males from Lexington introduces bias, as it over-represents males and Lexington residents, who may have higher incomes. A simple random sample would give every possible sample of 1000 residents an equal chance of selection, achieved by randomly selecting individuals from the entire state, ensuring proportional representation of gender and location.

Example 3: Types of Random Sampling

Identify the type of random sampling used in each scenario:

  1. Selecting 10 students by randomly choosing one row in a classroom with 5 rows of 10 students each.
  2. Dividing surgeons by gender, then randomly sampling 50 males and 50 females.
  3. Choosing every 7th inmate from a list of prison inmates.
  4. Randomly selecting 30 elementary schools in New York City and sampling all students in those schools.

1. Cluster Sampling: Selecting one row (a cluster) means only certain combinations of 10 students are possible, not all samples of size 10, ruling out a simple random sample.

2. Stratified Sampling: Dividing by gender (strata) and sampling equally from each ensures balanced representation.

3. Systematic Sampling: Selecting every 7th inmate from a list, but alphabetical ordering may introduce bias.

4. Cluster Sampling: Schools are clusters, and randomly selecting 30 schools simplifies data collection while maintaining randomness.

Example 4: Sampling Error

A random sample of 1600 adults finds that 800 enjoy amusement park rides. Calculate:

  1. The sample proportion of adults who enjoy amusement park rides.
  2. The margin of error.
  3. A 95% confidence statement for the population proportion.

1. Sample proportion: \( \hat{p} = \frac{800}{1600} = 0.5 \) or 50%.

2. Margin of error: \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{1600}} = \frac{1}{40} = 0.025 \) or 2.5%.

3. We are 95% confident that the population proportion of adults who enjoy amusement park rides is between 47.5% and 52.5%.
