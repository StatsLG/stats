Sampling Distribution of \(\bar{x}\)

Estimating the Population Mean

The statistic \(\bar{x}\) (sample mean) estimates \(\mu\) (population mean). In this class, we focus on "good" statistics, meaning that with proper techniques, the sampling distribution is centered at the parameter and has low variability. Understanding these characteristics helps us derive key facts about the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x}\).

Key Properties

  • \(\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu\)
    The average value of \(\bar{x}\) across all possible samples equals \(\mu\), the population mean.
  • \(\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
    The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x}\) is the population standard deviation \(\sigma\) divided by the square root of the sample size \(n\).

Standard Error: The standard deviation of the sampling distribution (\(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)) is called the standard error of \(\bar{x}\).

Normal Distribution Case

If the sample comes from a normal population with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), then: \[ Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \] has a standard normal distribution.

Central Limit Theorem

For any population with mean \(\mu\) and standard deviation \(\sigma\), if \(n \geq 30\): \[ Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}} \] is approximately standard normal.

Examples

Example 1

A population of soft drink cans has amounts of liquid following a normal distribution with \(\mu = 12\) oz and \(\sigma = 0.2\) oz.

  1. What is the probability that a single can is between 11.9 and 12.1 oz?
  2. What is the probability that \(\bar{x}\) is between 11.9 and 12.1 oz for \(n = 16\) cans?

1. For a single can (\(x\)):

\( Z_1 = \frac{11.9 - 12}{0.2} = -0.5 \), \( Z_2 = \frac{12.1 - 12}{0.2} = 0.5 \)

Using standard normal tables: \( P(-0.5 < Z < 0.5) = P(Z < 0.5) - P(Z < -0.5) = 0.6915 - 0.3085 = 0.3830 \)

2. For \(\bar{x}\) with \(n = 16\):

Standard error: \(\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{0.2}{\sqrt{16}} = \frac{0.2}{4} = 0.05\)

\( Z_1 = \frac{11.9 - 12}{0.05} = -2 \), \( Z_2 = \frac{12.1 - 12}{0.05} = 2 \)

\( P(-2 < Z < 2) = P(Z < 2) - P(Z < -2) = 0.9772 - 0.0228 = 0.9544 \)

Example 2

A population of trees has heights with a mean of 110 feet and a standard deviation of 20 feet. Suppose a sample of 100 trees is selected. Find the following:

  1. \(\mu_{\bar{x}}\)
  2. \(\sigma_{\bar{x}}\)
  3. \(P(\bar{x} > 108)\)
  4. What about \(P(X > 108)\)?

1. \(\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu = 110\) feet

2. \(\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{20}{\sqrt{100}} = \frac{20}{10} = 2\) feet

3. \(P(\bar{x} > 108)\): \( Z = \frac{108 - 110}{2} = -1 \), \( P(Z > -1) = 1 - P(Z < -1) = 1 - 0.1587 = 0.8413 \)

4. \(P(X > 108)\) (single tree): \( Z = \frac{108 - 110}{20} = -0.1 \), \( P(Z > -0.1) = 1 - P(Z < -0.1) = 1 - 0.4602 = 0.5398 \)
