Hypothesis Testing

General Setup of a Hypothesis Test

Statistical hypotheses are statements about population parameters. In statistics, we test one hypothesis against another. The Alternative Hypothesis (\(H_a\)) is the hypothesis we want to prove, while the Null Hypothesis (\(H_0\)) is the hypothesis that contradicts \(H_a\). In a hypothesis test, we test the null hypothesis, attempting to prove it false with a high level of confidence.

After collecting sample data, we make one of two decisions:

  1. Reject \(H_0\) (and believe \(H_a\))
  2. Fail to reject \(H_0\) (due to insufficient evidence to reject it)

Types of Errors

There are two types of errors in a hypothesis test:

  • Type I Error: Reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is true. The probability of this error is the Level of Significance, denoted \(\alpha\).
  • Type II Error: Fail to reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is false. The probability of this error is denoted \(\beta\).

The Level of Significance (\(\alpha\)) represents the risk of a Type I error. For example, if \(\alpha = 0.05\), rejecting \(H_0\) means we are 95% confident in concluding \(H_a\) (where \(1 - \alpha\) is the confidence level).

Power (\(1 - \beta\)) is the probability of rejecting \(H_0\) when it is false (i.e., correctly proving \(H_a\)). While power is complex to calculate, itâ€™s important to understand its relationship with errors and confidence.

Hypothesis tests are similar to dichotomous tests (introduced in section III.B.). Both have two possible outcomes (Reject \(H_0\) or Fail to Reject \(H_0\)). The Type I error (\(\alpha\)) is analogous to the false positive rate, and the Type II error (\(\beta\)) is analogous to the false negative rate. Power is similar to sensitivity, and the confidence level is similar to specificity.

Managing Errors

In inferential statistics, we aim for small \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\). However, decreasing \(\alpha\) increases \(\beta\) if all else is constant, and vice versa. Increasing the sample size is the best way to reduce both errors simultaneously.

Test Statistic and p-value

The Test Statistic is computed to make the decision in a hypothesis test, based on the known sampling distribution. The p-value is the smallest \(\alpha\) at which \(H_0\) would be rejected. Decision rules are:

  • If \(p\)-value \(\leq \alpha\), Reject \(H_0\).
  • If \(p\)-value \(> \alpha\), Fail to Reject \(H_0\).

Steps for a Hypothesis Test

  1. State \(H_a\) and \(H_0\).
  2. Calculate the test statistic.
  3. Identify the p-value.
  4. Make a decision.
  5. Interpret the result.

Examples

Below are example statements of statistical hypotheses:

  • Mean weight of adult males is greater than 160 pounds (\(H_a: \mu > 160\)).
  • Proportion of students with a 4.0 GPA is less than 0.01 (\(H_a: p < 0.01\)).

For each example, the null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) would contradict the alternative hypothesis. For instance:

  • For the mean weight: \(H_0: \mu \leq 160\).
  • For the GPA proportion: \(H_0: p \geq 0.01\).

These examples illustrate how to set up hypotheses for testing. To perform a full hypothesis test, you would follow the steps outlined in the Theory tab, including calculating a test statistic and p-value based on sample data.
