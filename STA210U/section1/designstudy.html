Conducting a Statistical Study

Designing and Interpreting Research

Theory

Variables in a Study

Response Variable (Dependent): Measures the outcome of a study.

Explanatory Variable (Independent): Attempts to explain or cause changes in the response variable.

Lurking Variable: Affects the response variable but is not included in the study.

Confounding Variable: Its effect on the response variable cannot be separated from the explanatory variable’s effect.

Observational Study vs. Experiment

Experiment: Creates differences in the explanatory variable and examines results, often using:

  • Random Assignment: Assigns subjects to treatment groups by chance (e.g., coin flip).
  • Control Group: Receives no treatment or a placebo to control lurking variables.
  • Placebo: Mimics the treatment to blind subjects.
  • Blinding: Single (subjects unaware of treatment) or double (neither subjects nor administrators know).
  • Completely Randomized Design: All subjects randomly assigned to treatment groups.
  • Block Design: Random assignment within groups (blocks) similar in a characteristic affecting the response.

Observational Study: Observes differences in the explanatory variable and their relation to the response variable, without assigning treatments. Confounding is harder to control.

Measurement

Sample Survey: Collects data by asking questions and recording responses, common in observational studies.

Survey Question Guidelines:

  • Avoid unnecessary complexity; keep questions simple and specific.
  • Ensure neutrality to avoid leading respondents.
  • Randomize question order to prevent bias.
  • Be aware of respondents’ desire to please.
  • Open-Ended Questions: Allow free responses but are hard to summarize.
  • Closed-Ended Questions: Provide response options, easier to analyze but limit ideas.

Validity: A measure is valid if it measures what it claims (e.g., rates are often more valid than frequencies).

Reliability: A measure is reliable if it is consistent across repeated measurements.

Non-sampling Error

Errors not due to sampling include:

  • Measurement Error: Errors in measuring outcomes.
  • Response Error: Incorrect or careless responses.
  • Processing Error: Data entry or calculation mistakes.
  • Loss of Subjects: Non-adherers (not following treatment) or dropouts (not completing the study).
  • Non-response: Failure of subjects to respond, reducing representativeness. Response rate = \( \frac{\text{Number of Responses}}{\text{Total Sampled}} \).

Interpretation of Results

Statistically Significant: An effect unlikely to occur by chance, indicating its existence in the population.

Practically Significant: An effect large enough to be meaningful, not guaranteed by statistical significance.

Interaction: When the effect of one explanatory variable on the response depends on another explanatory variable.

Believing a Statistic

Key considerations for evaluating research:

  • Funding and conductor of the research (avoid conflicts of interest).
  • Type of study (observational, experiment, simulation).
  • Selection and characteristics of subjects.
  • Presence of bias or non-sampling errors.
  • Sample size and reported sampling error.
  • Control of lurking and confounding variables.
  • Random assignment and group comparability.
  • Magnitude and practical significance of effects.
  • Appropriateness of analysis techniques.

Examples

Example 1: Variables in a Study

In a study examining the effect of drug dosage on blood pressure reduction, identify the response and explanatory variables, and suggest a potential lurking variable.

Response Variable: Blood pressure reduction (depends on the drug).

Explanatory Variable: Drug dosage (causes changes in blood pressure).

Lurking Variable: Diet, as it may affect blood pressure but is not part of the study.

Example 2: Observational Study vs. Experiment

Classify each study as an observational study or experiment, and explain why:

  1. Researchers give a drug to one group and a placebo to another to measure pain relief.
  2. Researchers survey teachers to see if years of experience relate to student test scores.

1. Experiment: Researchers assign the drug or placebo (explanatory variable) and measure pain relief (response variable).

2. Observational Study: Researchers observe years of experience (explanatory variable) and test scores (response variable) without assigning treatments.

Example 3: Measurement

Evaluate the validity and reliability of measuring U.S. homicides from 1970 to present using:

  1. The number of homicides.
  2. A scale that always weighs 10 pounds too heavy.

1. Number of homicides: Not valid (population growth affects counts, not reflecting true trends); reliability depends on consistent data collection.

2. Scale: Reliable (consistently 10 pounds heavy) but not valid (inaccurate measurements).

Example 4: Non-sampling Error

A survey of 3200 people yields 1200 responses. Calculate the response rate and discuss its implications.

Response Rate: \( \frac{1200}{3200} = 0.375 \) or 37.5%.

Implications: A 37.5% response rate is low (below 80%), suggesting potential bias, as responders may differ from non-responders on the variable of interest, reducing representativeness.

Example 5: Interpretation and Believing a Statistic

A study finds a statistically significant difference in test scores between online and in-class students. What questions should you ask to evaluate the results?

Key questions include:

  • Was the study an experiment with random assignment or an observational study?
  • Was there a control for lurking variables (e.g., student motivation)?
  • Is the difference practically significant, or is it small despite statistical significance?
  • What was the sample size, and was the sampling method random?
  • Who funded or conducted the study, and is there a conflict of interest?
