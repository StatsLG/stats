Introduction to Statistics and Data Collection

Foundations of Statistical Analysis

Theory

Basic Statistical Terminology

Statistics is the study of how to collect, organize, analyze, and interpret information. It provides a process for estimation and decision-making under uncertainty without bias. Key terms include:

  • Population: The entire collection of individuals or items of interest.
  • Sample: A subset of the population on which measurements are taken.
  • Data: The measurements collected from the sample.
  • Individuals: The objects (people, animals, or things) described by the data.
  • Variable: A characteristic of an individual that varies across individuals.
  • Statistic: A numerical characteristic of a sample, which varies from sample to sample.
  • Parameter: A fixed numerical characteristic of a population, known only through a perfect census.
  • Census: Measurements taken from the entire population.

Branches of Statistics

Statistics is divided into two main branches:

  • Descriptive Statistics: Methods to summarize data without error, as they describe the data at hand.
  • Inferential Statistics: Methods to make inferences about a population based on a sample, involving error that can be controlled and calculated using probability.

Types of Studies

Common types of studies include:

  • Observational Study: Observes individuals and measures variables without influencing responses.
  • Simulation: A numerical model of real-world phenomena, often used for complex or dangerous scenarios.
  • Experiment: Imposes treatments on individuals to observe responses, studying cause-and-effect relationships.

Experiment Terminology: Includes subjects (individuals studied), treatment (conditions applied), and treatment diagram (a table showing treatment combinations).

Types of Data

Data is classified as:

  • Qualitative (Categorical): Non-numerical data that categorizes individuals (e.g., gender).
  • Quantitative: Numerical data with meaningful values, either discrete (counted, e.g., number of children) or continuous (measured, e.g., height).

Levels of Measurement

Variables are measured at different levels:

  • Nominal: Identifies categories without order (e.g., gender).
  • Ordinal: Categories with order but no meaningful differences (e.g., rankings).
  • Interval: Ordered data with meaningful differences but no true zero (e.g., temperature in Fahrenheit).
  • Ratio: Ordered data with meaningful differences and a true zero (e.g., height, weight).

Examples

Example 1: Terminology

Suppose we want to estimate the average income for all residents of Kentucky by selecting 1000 people and collecting their incomes. Identify the following:

  1. What is the population?
  2. What is the sample?
  3. What is the data?
  4. Who are the individuals?
  5. What is the variable?
  6. What is the parameter?
  7. What is the statistic?
  8. If we measured all residents, what would this be called?

1. All residents of the state of Kentucky

2. The 1000 people selected from the state

3. The list of the 1000 incomes collected from the sample

4. Each person in the sample

5. Income (varies across individuals)

6. The average income for all residents of Kentucky

7. The average income for the 1000 people selected

8. A census

Example 2: Branches of Statistics

Classify each as a descriptive or inferential statistic:

  1. A basketball player’s free throw percentage based on performance up to the midpoint of the season.
  2. The average GPA for all current students based on complete data from the records office.
  3. The president’s approval rating based on a survey of 5,000 U.S. residents.
  4. A graph showing all sales this year for major vehicle manufacturers.
    5. –

    1. Descriptive (no error, based on known performance data)

    2. Descriptive (no error, uses complete population data)

    3. Inferential (error exists, estimates population approval using a sample)

    4. Descriptive (no error, shows all sales data)

Example 3: Types of Studies

Identify each study as an observational study, experiment, or simulation:

  1. Patients are given a drug at various dosage levels to study effectiveness.
  2. Various vehicles are studied to see which are safest in a head-on collision.
  3. The outcome of a presidential election is predicted by asking individuals over the telephone.
  4. Change a container from Pepsi to Coke and calculate the percentage of people who notice.

1. Experiment (drug dosage is imposed to measure effects)

2. Simulation (uses models to simulate crashes, avoiding real harm)

3. Observational (surveys without influencing responses)

4. Experiment (changes container to measure noticeability)

Example 4: Types of Data and Levels of Measurement

Identify the data type (qualitative or quantitative) and level of measurement for each:

  1. Gender
  2. Time to run a mile
  3. Number of soccer goals scored
  4. Temperature in Fahrenheit

1. Qualitative, Nominal

2. Quantitative (Continuous), Ratio

3. Quantitative (Discrete), Ratio

4. Quantitative (Continuous), Interval
