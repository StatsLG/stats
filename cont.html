Density Curves

Continuous Random Variables

A density curve is a smooth curve that provides the most common representation of a population's distribution. Statistical software can transform the separate bars of a histogram into this smooth curve, effectively capturing the overall shape of the distribution.

The shape of a density curve is determined using methods similar to those applied to histograms, making it a familiar tool for visualizing data distributions.

Density curves are particularly valuable for determining the proportion, percentage, or probability of a population falling within a specific interval (note that these terms represent the same value). This differs from discrete random variables, where probability is assigned to individual points. In contrast, for a continuous random variable, probability is assigned to intervals.

These curves are designed so that the area under the curve directly represents the proportion, percentage, or probability of observations, providing a clear visual and mathematical representation of the data.

Properties of a Valid Density Curve

To represent a continuous probability distribution accurately, a density curve must satisfy specific properties, much like the requirements for a valid discrete probability distribution:

  • Continuity: The curve must be continuous, as it represents a continuous random variable.
  • Non-negativity: The curve must never fall below the x-axis, since probability cannot be less than zero.
  • Total Area: The total area under the curve must equal 1, reflecting that the total probability in any distribution is 1 and cannot exceed it.
