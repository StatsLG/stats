Section 3 — Variability and Relative Standing

A measure of dispersion tells how spread out the data are. Two data sets can have the same mean/median/mode yet very different variability—so we always pair a measure of center with a measure of spread.

Same Center, Different Spread

Two distributions can have identical mean, median, mode, and midrange, but one be more variable. That’s why dispersion matters when describing a data set.

Sample Range

Distance between highest and lowest data values: \(\text{Range} = \text{High} - \text{Low}\).

Example — Years of Experience

Data: 1, 30, 22, 10, 5 → Range \(= 30 - 1 = 29\).

  • Easy to compute.
  • Totally sensitive to extremes (uses only the two outer points).

Sample Variance \(s^2\)

Average squared distance from the mean.

Conceptual formula

\[ s^2 \;=\; \frac{\sum (x-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} \]

Computational formula

\[ s^2 \;=\; \frac{n\sum x^2 - (\sum x)^2}{n(n-1)} \]

The numerator \(\sum (x-\bar{x})^2\) is the sum of squares (SS or SSX). The two formulas are algebraically equivalent; the computational version is often easier by hand.

Variance characteristics

  • Involves all data points directly.
  • Units are squared → can be hard to interpret.
  • Never negative.
  • Larger \(s^2\) ⇒ more variability.

Why not average the raw distances?

If you try \(\tfrac{1}{n}\sum (x-\bar{x})\), it’s always 0 (positives and negatives cancel around the mean). Algebraically, \(\sum (x-\bar{x}) = \sum x - n\bar{x} = 0\). Squaring prevents the cancellation.

Sample Standard Deviation \(s\)

\(s = \sqrt{s^2}\). Same units as the data and the most common measure of spread.

Example — Years of Experience

\(s = \sqrt{146.3} \approx 12.095\).

Standard Deviation as a Unit (z-scores)

\(z = \dfrac{x-\bar{x}}{s}\) measures how many standard deviations an observation is above/below the mean.

Example

Suppose \(\bar{x}=79\) and \(s=9\).

  • For \(x=88\): \(z=\dfrac{88-79}{9}=1\) → 1 SD above mean.
  • For \(x=63\): \(z=\dfrac{63-79}{9}=-\dfrac{16}{9}\approx -1.778\) → 1.778 SD below mean.

Empirical Rule (mound-shaped distributions)

  • \(\approx 68\%\) within \((\bar{x}-s,\;\bar{x}+s)\)
  • \(\approx 95\%\) within \((\bar{x}-2s,\;\bar{x}+2s)\)
  • \(\approx 99.7\%\) within \((\bar{x}-3s,\;\bar{x}+3s)\)

Example — “12 oz.” Pepsi cans

\(\bar{x}=12\ \text{oz},\ s=0.1\ \text{oz}\)

  • 68%: \((11.9,\ 12.1)\)
  • 95%: \((11.8,\ 12.2)\)
  • 99.7%: \((11.7,\ 12.3)\)

Measures of Position

Percentiles give the percent below an observation (e.g., 95th percentile means better than 95% of peers).

Quartiles split data into four equal parts.

  • \(Q_1\) (25th percentile): 25% below, 75% above.
  • \(Q_2\) (50th percentile, the median \(\tilde{x}\)): 50% below/above.
  • \(Q_3\) (75th percentile): 75% below, 25% above.

Procedure

  1. Order the data (smallest to largest).
  2. Find \(\tilde{x}=Q_2\).
  3. \(Q_1\): median of the lower half (not including \(Q_2\)).
  4. \(Q_3\): median of the upper half (not including \(Q_2\)).

Quartile Examples

  • Data #1: 1–6 → \(Q_1=2,\ Q_2=3.5,\ Q_3=5\)
  • Data #2: 1–7 → \(Q_1=2,\ Q_2=4,\ Q_3=6\)
  • Data #3: 1–8 → \(Q_1=2.5,\ Q_2=4.5,\ Q_3=6.5\)
  • Data #4: 1–9 → \(Q_1=2.5,\ Q_2=5,\ Q_3=7.5\)

Five-Number Summary & IQR

Five-number summary: \(\text{Low},\ Q_1,\ Q_2,\ Q_3,\ \text{High}\).

Interquartile Range (IQR): middle 50% spread, \( \text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1 \).

  • IQR isn’t affected by extremes (unlike the range).
  • Still, \(s\) is usually preferred for most analyses.

Box & Whisker Plots (Boxplots)

Based on the five-number summary; great for comparing a quantitative variable across groups.

How a boxplot is constructed

  • Scale on the axis from Low to High.
  • Draw a box from \(Q_1\) to \(Q_3\); a line inside at \(Q_2\) (median).
  • Whiskers extend to the smallest/largest non-extreme values.
  • Extreme values shown as points or asterisks.

From the “money” example: faculty have higher typical values (box and median higher) and more variability (larger box/whiskers). The 73 is flagged as an extreme.

What to read from a boxplot: (i) typical value (median/box position), (ii) variability (box size/whiskers), (iii) shape (via median position and whisker lengths).

Identifying Shape from Boxplots

  • Symmetric: median in the center of the box; whiskers similar length (e.g., bell-shaped, uniform).
  • Left-skewed: median right of center; longer left whisker.
  • Right-skewed: median left of center; longer right whisker.

Tip: mentally rotate boxplots 90° clockwise so the scale increases left→right (like a histogram) to judge skew consistently.

Example — Tread Design vs Stopping Distance

  • Best (shortest stopping): Design B → its box/median sit lower on the axis.
  • Most consistent: Design C → smallest box/whiskers (though consistently worse here).
  • Shape: All are right-skewed (median left of center; longer right whisker).
