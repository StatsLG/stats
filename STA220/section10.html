Identify Correlation Graphically

A scatterplot displays the relationship between two quantitative variables: \(x\) on the horizontal axis (independent / explanatory) and \(y\) on the vertical axis (dependent / response). In data, multiple individuals can share the same \(x\) but have different \(y\), so we look for patterns, not a single function that passes through all points.

Kinds of Correlation

Perfect Positive Linear

All points exactly on a line with positive slope.

Perfect Negative Linear

All points exactly on a line with negative slope.

Positive Linear

Points cluster about a line with positive slope (typical in practice).

Negative Linear

Points cluster about a line with negative slope.

Non-Linear

Clear pattern but not linear (e.g., quadratic). For this class, call it non-linear.

No Correlation

No apparent pattern; \(r\) near 0 for linear association.

The Pearson Correlation Coefficient \(r\)

\(r\) measures the direction (sign) and strength (magnitude) of a linear relationship. Range: \(-1 \le r \le 1\).

Variance components

\[ SS_X=\sum(x-\bar x)^2,\quad SS_Y=\sum(y-\bar y)^2,\quad S_{XY}=\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y). \]

\[ r=\frac{S_{XY}}{\sqrt{SS_X\,SS_Y}} \;=\; \frac{\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)} {\sqrt{\sum(x-\bar x)^2\;\sum(y-\bar y)^2}} \;=\; \frac{n\sum xy-\left(\sum x\right)\!\left(\sum y\right)} {\sqrt{\big(n\sum x^2-(\sum x)^2\big)\,\big(n\sum y^2-(\sum y)^2\big)}}. \]

Derivation snippets (as used in class)

\[ SS_X=\sum(x-\bar x)^2 =\sum x^2-\frac{(\sum x)^2}{n},\qquad SS_Y=\sum(y-\bar y)^2 =\sum y^2-\frac{(\sum y)^2}{n}, \] \[ S_{XY}=\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y) =\sum xy-\frac{(\sum x)(\sum y)}{n}. \]

Interpretation

  • \(r>0\): \(x\) and \(y\) rise together (positive slope).
  • \(r<0\): one rises as the other falls (negative slope).
  • \(|r|\) close to 1: strong linear fit. Rule of thumb here: strong if \(|r|\ge 0.75\).

Reminder

\(r\) only measures linear association; a strong non-linear relationship can still have \(r\approx0\).

Correlation Does Not Imply Causation

Example: ice cream consumption and assaults both rise in summer—strong \(r\) but due to a lurking variable (temperature). Correlation alone never proves cause–effect.

The Simple Linear Regression Model

Regression: predict a response \(y\) from an explanatory \(x\).

Linear regression model: \( \hat y=b_0+b_1x \). Here \(b_1\) is the slope and \(b_0\) is the \(y\)-intercept. Variation in \(y\) around the line appears as vertical distances (“errors”).

Only use a linear model when the scatterplot and \(r\) suggest a strong linear relationship.

Least-Squares Line: Formulas

Method of Least Squares: choose the line that minimizes the sum of squared vertical distances.

Closed forms

\[ b_1=\frac{S_{XY}}{SS_X} =\frac{\sum(x-\bar x)(y-\bar y)}{\sum(x-\bar x)^2} =\frac{n\sum xy-(\sum x)(\sum y)}{\,n\sum x^2-(\sum x)^2\,}, \] \[ b_0=\bar y-b_1\bar x =\frac{\sum y-b_1\sum x}{n}. \]

Note: the numerator of \(b_1\) matches the numerator of \(r\); their signs always agree.

Fitting, Prediction, & Residuals

Fit for Drug–BP data

\[ b_1=\frac{59{,}000}{500{,}000}=0.118,\qquad b_0=\frac{160-0.118(1500)}{5}=-3.4. \] Regression equation: \( \hat y=-3.4+0.118x \).

Predict

At \(x=250\): \( \hat y=-3.4+0.118(250)=26.1\).

Residual

Residual \(e=y-\hat y\). For the point \((400,44)\):

\(e=44-(-3.4+0.118\cdot 400)=44-43.8=0.2\) (point is 0.2 above the line).

\(R^2\) and Software Output

\(R^2\) (0–100%) is the percent of variation in \(y\) explained by the model. For simple linear regression, \(R^2=r^2\).

  • Drug–BP example: \(r=0.99728 \Rightarrow R^2\approx 99.5\%\).
  • From output, you can recover \(r=\pm\sqrt{R^2}\) — choose sign from the slope: positive slope ⇒ \(r>0\), negative ⇒ \(r<0\).

Cautions: Interpolation, Extrapolation, Outliers

Interpolation vs Extrapolation

Interpolation: predict within observed \(x\)-range (appropriate).

Extrapolation: predict beyond that range (unsafe with a basic model).

Extreme Values (Outliers)

\(r\) and the least-squares line are highly sensitive to outliers.

  • Example 1: \(R^2=0.0\%\Rightarrow r=0\) (no linear relationship).
  • Example 2: Add one extreme point → \(R^2=77.9\%\Rightarrow r=\sqrt{.779}=.883\) (apparently “strong” due entirely to the outlier).

Investigate and consider removing extreme points before concluding a relationship.
