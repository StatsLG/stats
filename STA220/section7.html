The Normal Distribution

  • Bell-shaped, symmetric about the mean \(\mu\); spread controlled by \(\sigma\).
  • For a normal, mean = median = mode.

Density: \[ f(x)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}\, e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}} \] Parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\) determine which normal curve we have.

The Standard Normal (z) & Table

Set \(\mu=0,\ \sigma=1\). The variable is \(Z\). The standard normal table we use gives \( P(0\le Z \le z) \).

Example A

\(P(0\le Z\le 1.55)=0.4394\)

Look up 1.5 (row) and 0.05 (column).

Example B

\(P(0\le Z\le 1.96)=0.4750\)

Look up 1.9 (row) and 0.06 (column).

Facts

  • Total area \(=1\).
  • Symmetric about 0 → \(P(Z<0)=P(Z>0)=0.5\).

From Any Normal to Standard Normal

If \(X\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\), then \[ Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \] is standard normal. This rescales the horizontal axis; areas are preserved.

Picture idea: mark \(\mu\), then \(\mu\pm\sigma\), \(\mu\pm2\sigma\), \(\mu\pm3\sigma\). The same bell, with a \(z\)-axis relabeled in SD units (0, ±1, ±2, ±3).

Sampling Distributions — Concepts

  • Population: all measurements (group of interest)
  • Sample: subset used for analysis
  • Parameter: numerical characteristic of a population (\(\mu,p,\sigma,\dots\))
  • Statistic: numerical characteristic of a sample (\(\bar x,\ \hat p,\ s,\dots\))

Because different random samples give different \(\bar x\), \(\bar x\) is a random variable. Its probability model over all possible samples is the sampling distribution.

Good estimators have: (a) center at the parameter (unbiased), (b) small variability (minimum variance among unbiased options).

\(\bar x\): Mean, Variance, SE & Illustration

Facts for \(\bar x\)

  • \(\mu_{\bar x}=\mu\)
  • \(\sigma^2_{\bar x}=\dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\)
  • \(\sigma_{\bar x}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt n}\) (the standard error)

Finite Population Correction (FPC)

Sampling without replacement from population \(N\):

\(\displaystyle \sigma^2_{\bar x}=\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\left(\frac{N-n}{N-1}\right)\)

(For large \(N\), FPC \(\approx1\).)

Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

If the population is normal, then for any \(n\): \[ Z=\frac{\bar x-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt n}\ \text{is standard normal.} \]

If the population is not known to be normal, the CLT says the distribution of \(\bar x\) is approximately normal for large \(n\) (use \(n\ge 30\) in this class).

Sampling Distribution of \(\hat p\)

Population proportion \(p=\dfrac{\#\text{ in population with characteristic}}{\#\text{ in population}}\). Sample proportion \(\hat p=\dfrac{\#\text{ in sample with characteristic}}{\#\text{ in sample}}\).

  • \(\mu_{\hat p}=p\) (unbiased)
  • \(\sigma^2_{\hat p}=\dfrac{pq}{n}\), with \(q=1-p\)
  • \(\sigma_{\hat p}=\sqrt{\dfrac{pq}{n}}\) (standard error)
  • For large \(n\), \(\displaystyle Z=\frac{\hat p-p}{\sqrt{pq/n}}\) is approximately standard normal.
