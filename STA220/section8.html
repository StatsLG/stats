Section 8 — Confidence Intervals Based on a Single Sample

Confidence, Tails, and \( \alpha \)

  • Level of significance \( \alpha \): chance we’re wrong.
  • Confidence level \(1-\alpha\): chance we’re correct.
  • One-tailed test: error in one tail; two-tailed: \(\alpha/2\) in each tail.
  • When constructing confidence intervals, we always use two tails (possible error on both sides).

If the sampling distribution is normal, the critical values are \(z_{\alpha}\) (one tail) and \(z_{\alpha/2}\) (two tails), where the tail area equals the subscript.

Common \(z_{\alpha/2}\) Values

90%: \(z_{\alpha/2}=z_{.05}=1.645\)

95%: \(z_{\alpha/2}=z_{.025}=1.96\)

99%: \(z_{\alpha/2}=z_{.005}=2.576\)

Example: at 95% confidence, the central area is 0.95, so 2.5% lies in each tail → between \(-1.96\) and \(1.96\) there is 95% of the area.

Why not 100%?

To capture 100% of a normal curve, \(z_{\alpha/2}\to\infty\); the CI would be \((-\infty,\infty)\), which is useless. Useful CIs have confidence < 100%.

Point vs Interval Estimates

A sample mean \( \bar{x} \) is a point estimate of \( \mu \), but for continuous models \(P(\bar{x}=\mu)=0\). We prefer interval estimates (confidence intervals) to reflect uncertainty: statistic ± margin of error.

CI for \( \mu \) with Known \( \sigma \)

When \( \sigma \) is known, the standardized statistic \[ Z=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \] is (approximately) standard normal. Using the middle \(1-\alpha\) area: \[ P\!\left(-z_{\alpha/2}

Algebra (solve for \( \mu \)) yields: \[ P\!\left(\bar{x}-z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \;<\; \mu \;<\; \bar{x}+z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)=1-\alpha. \] The margin of error is \(E=z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} = z\left(\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\).

How \(n\), Confidence, and Variability Affect \(E\)

  • Increase \(n\) ⟶ \(E\) decreases ⟶ narrower CI.
  • Increase confidence \((1-\alpha)\) ⟶ \(z_{\alpha/2}\) increases ⟶ \(E\) increases ⟶ wider CI.
  • Increase population variability \((\sigma)\) ⟶ \(E\) increases ⟶ wider CI.

Worked Example — Known \( \sigma \)

Visa account unpaid balances

\(n=100\), \( \bar{x}=645\), \( \sigma=132\).

95% CI

\(E=1.96\left(\frac{132}{\sqrt{100}}\right)=25.87\)

\(645\pm25.87=(619.13,\ 670.87)\)

Interpretation: We are 95% confident the mean unpaid balance is between \$619.13 and \$670.87.

99% CI

\(E=2.576\left(\frac{132}{\sqrt{100}}\right)=34.00\)

\(645\pm34.00=(611.00,\ 679.00)\)

Interpretation: We are 99% confident the mean unpaid balance is between \$611.00 and \$679.00.

CI for \( \mu \) with Unknown \( \sigma \) (t-Distribution)

Replace \( \sigma \) by the sample SD \(s\). Then \[ t=\frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}} \] has a t-distribution with \(df=n-1\) (exact if sampling from a normal population; for large \(n\), t \(\approx\) z).

Two-tailed t-interval: \[ E=t_{\alpha/2,\;df}\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\right),\qquad \bar{x}\pm E. \]

Using the t Table

  • Notation: \(t_{\alpha,\;df}\) (one tail) and \(t_{\alpha/2,\;df}\) (two tails).
  • Degrees of freedom \(df=n-1\).
  • For \(df=1\) to \(30\), exact rows; for \(30120\), use the bottom row (\(df=\infty\)), which equals the corresponding z values.

Worked t-Interval Examples

Tire mileage (thousands of miles)

Sample: 42, 36, 46, 43, 41, 35, 43, 45, 40, 39

\(n=10\), \( \bar{x}=41\), \(s=3.590\); 95% confidence → \(\alpha/2=0.025\), \(df=9\), \(t_{.025,9}=2.262\).

\(E=2.262\left(\frac{3.590}{\sqrt{10}}\right)=2.568\)

CI: \(41\pm 2.568=(38.432,\ 43.568)\)

Interpretation: We are 95% confident the mean tire mileage is between 38,432 and 43,568 miles.

Apple weights (oz.)

\(n=20\), \( \bar{x}=9.2\), \(s=1.1\); 99% confidence → \(\alpha/2=0.005\), \(df=19\), \(t_{.005,19}=2.861\).

\(E=2.861\left(\frac{1.1}{\sqrt{20}}\right)=0.704\)

CI: \(9.2\pm 0.704=(8.496,\ 9.904)\)

Interpretation: We are 99% confident the mean apple weight is between 8.496 and 9.904 oz.

CI for a Proportion \( p \) (large \(n\))

For large samples, \( \displaystyle Z=\frac{\hat{p}-p}{\sqrt{pq/n}} \) is approximately standard normal. We estimate \(p\) and \(q=1-p\) by \( \hat{p} \) and \( \hat{q}=1-\hat{p} \).

Margin of error and CI: \[ E=z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}\,\hat{q}}{n}},\qquad \hat{p}\pm E. \]

Worked Example — Voters

Survey: \(n=1200\), “Republican” responses = 540 → \(\hat{p}=540/1200=0.45\), \(\hat{q}=0.55\); 95% → \(z_{\alpha/2}=1.96\).

\(E=1.96\sqrt{\frac{(0.45)(0.55)}{1200}}=0.028\) (2.8%)

CI: \(45\%\pm2.8\%=(42.2\%,\,47.8\%)\)

Interpretation: We are 95% confident the true percentage is between 42.2% and 47.8%.

Planning \(n\): Sample Size for a Target Margin

Solve \(E=z_{\alpha/2}\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\) for \(n\): \[ n=\left(\frac{z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma}{E}\right)^2. \] (When planning, \( \sigma \) is estimated via a pilot study or prior data.) Always round up.

Worked Example — Physician hours

Target: 99% confidence (\(z_{.005}=2.576\)), margin \(E=1\) hour, SD estimate \( \sigma=11.97\).

\(n=\left(\dfrac{2.576\cdot 11.97}{1}\right)^2=950.779 \Rightarrow 951\) (round up)
