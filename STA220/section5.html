Random Variables: Discrete vs Continuous

Discrete: values are separated points on the number line; finite or countable outcomes (typically counts).

  • # of students in a course
  • # of flips until first tail

Continuous: values fill intervals on the number line; uncountably many outcomes (typically measurements).

  • Height of a person
  • Distance traveled to work

For discrete \(X\), we assign a probability to every possible value \(x\).

Valid Discrete Distribution — Checklist

  1. \(x\) is discrete (finite or countable outcomes).
  2. \(0 \le P(x) \le 1\) for all \(x\).
  3. \(\sum P(x) = 1\).

Population Mean & Variance (Discrete)

Expected value (mean): \(\displaystyle \mu=\sum x\,P(x)\).

Variance: two equivalent forms \[ \sigma^2=\sum (x-\mu)^2 P(x) \quad=\quad \sum x^2 P(x) - \mu^2 \] and standard deviation: \(\sigma=\sqrt{\sigma^2}\).

Variance — Conceptual vs Computational

Show that \(\sum (x-\mu)^2 P(x)=\sum x^2 P(x)-\mu^2\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum (x-\mu)^2 P(x) &= \sum (x^2-2\mu x+\mu^2)P(x) \\ &= \sum x^2P(x) - 2\mu \sum xP(x) + \mu^2\sum P(x) \\ &= \sum x^2P(x) - 2\mu(\mu) + \mu^2(1) \\ &= \sum x^2P(x) - \mu^2. \end{aligned} \]

Why not drop the square?

\(\displaystyle \sum (x-\mu)P(x) = \sum xP(x) - \mu\sum P(x) = \mu - \mu = 0.\) Without squaring, positives and negatives cancel.

Binomial Distribution — Properties

  • \(n\) identical, independent trials.
  • Each trial → success \(S\) or failure \(F\).
  • \(P(S)=p,\;P(F)=q=1-p\) (same each trial).
  • Random variable \(X=\) # of successes in \(n\) trials.

Illustration with \(n=3\)

Outcomes via tree diagram (not equally likely unless \(p=1/2\)); use multiplication rule:

\(SSS\Rightarrow p^3\)

\(SSF\Rightarrow p^2 q\)

\(SFS\Rightarrow p^2 q\)

\(SFF\Rightarrow p q^2\)

\(FSS\Rightarrow p^2 q\)

\(FSF\Rightarrow p q^2\)

\(FFS\Rightarrow p q^2\)

\(FFF\Rightarrow q^3\)

Therefore \(P(X=0)=q^3,\;P(X=1)=3pq^2,\;P(X=2)=3p^2q,\;P(X=3)=p^3\).

Binomial Formula

\[ P(X=x)=\binom{n}{x} p^{\,x} q^{\,n-x} \]

Binomial Examples

10 fair coin flips; \(P(X=7)\)

\(n=10,\;p=1/2\)

\(P= \binom{10}{7}(1/2)^{7}(1/2)^{3}= \binom{10}{7}/2^{10}\approx 0.117\)

5 die rolls; \(X=\#\) of sixes; \(P(X=2)\)

\(n=5,\;p=1/6\)

\(P=\binom{5}{2}(1/6)^2(5/6)^3 \approx 0.161\)

Cancer remission

Remission in \(1/4\) of patients; 3 patients; \(P(X=2)\).

\(n=3,\;p=1/4\Rightarrow P=\binom{3}{2}(1/4)^2(3/4)^1\approx 0.1406\)

Inequalities via the Complement

For \(X\sim\text{Bin}(n=8,p=0.4)\), compute \(P(X\le 6)\) via complement:

\(P(X>6)=P(X=7)+P(X=8) = \binom{8}{7}0.4^{7}0.6^{1} + \binom{8}{8}0.4^{8}0.6^{0} \approx 0.007864 + 0.000655 = 0.008519\).

\(P(X\le 6)=1-0.008519=0.991481\).

Binomial Mean & Standard Deviation

\(\mu = np,\qquad \sigma^2 = npq,\qquad \sigma=\sqrt{npq}\).

Example: 100-item MCQ (4 choices)

Random guessing; \(n=100,\;p=1/4,\;q=3/4\).

  • \(\mu=np=100\cdot \tfrac14=25\) (expected correct \(=25\%\)).
  • \(\sigma^2=npq=100\cdot \tfrac14\cdot \tfrac34=18.75\).
  • \(\sigma=\sqrt{18.75}\approx 4.330\).
