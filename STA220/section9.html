General Setup of a Hypothesis Test

Statistical hypotheses are statements about population parameters, e.g. \( \mu>160 \) or \( p<0.01 \).

  • Alternative hypothesis \(H_a\): the claim we want to prove.
  • Null hypothesis \(H_0\): a contradicting statement tested against \(H_a\).

We test \(H_0\). After sampling, decide to Reject \(H_0\) (in favor of \(H_a\)) or Fail to Reject \(H_0\).

Errors, Confidence, and Power

  • Type I error: Reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is true (probability \( \alpha \)).
  • Type II error: Fail to Reject \(H_0\) when \(H_0\) is false (probability \( \beta \)).
  • Power: \(1-\beta\), probability of correctly rejecting a false \(H_0\).
  • Confidence level: \(1-\alpha\). If \( \alpha=0.05\), concluding \(H_a\) corresponds to 95% confidence.
  • Making \( \alpha \) smaller (holding all else fixed) makes \( \beta \) larger. Increasing sample size is how to reduce both.

Decision Table & Tradeoffs

Decision from the test \(H_0\) is false \(H_0\) is true
Reject \(H_0\) Correct (Power \(=1-\beta\)) Type I error (\(\alpha\))
Fail to Reject \(H_0\) Type II error (\(\beta\)) Correct (Confidence \(=1-\alpha\))

5 Steps of a Hypothesis Test

  1. State \(H_a\) and \(H_0\).
  2. Specify the critical region (depends on \(H_a\) and \( \alpha \)).
  3. Compute the test statistic.
  4. Make the decision (reject / fail to reject).
  5. Interpret the result in context.

Test of Hypothesis for \( \mu \) with Known \( \sigma \) (Z)

When \( \sigma \) is known, the sampling distribution is standard normal: \[ Z \;=\; \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}. \]

Example — Math Competency of Elementary Education Majors

Claim: mean score \(<35\). Use \( \alpha=0.05\). Given \( n=165,\ \bar{x}=32.91,\ \sigma=9.5\).

  1. \(H_a:\mu<35\);\; \(H_0:\mu\ge 35\).
  2. Left-tailed Z test; reject if \(Z\le -1.645\) (critical region has area \( \alpha \) on the left).
  3. \(\displaystyle Z=\frac{32.91-35}{9.5/\sqrt{165}}=-2.83\).
  4. Since \(-2.83\le-1.645\), Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Conclude (95% confidence) the mean score is less than 35.

The p-Value Approach

p-value: the smallest \( \alpha \) for which \(H_0\) would be rejected (area consistent with the observed test statistic).

Decision rule: If p-value \(\le \alpha\) → Reject \(H_0\); otherwise → Fail to Reject \(H_0\).

Example (same data)

Left tail area at \(Z=-2.83\): \( \text{p-value}=P(Z\le-2.83)=0.0023 \). Since \(0.0023\le0.05\), reject \(H_0\). Matches the traditional method.

Test of Hypothesis for \( \mu \) with Unknown \( \sigma \) (t)

Replace \( \sigma \) by the sample SD \( s \), giving a t-statistic with \( df=n-1 \): \[ t \;=\; \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}}. \]

Example — Post Grape Nuts (Two-Tailed t) & Matching CI

Prove packages are not 24 oz. Use \( \alpha=0.05\). Given \( n=100,\ \bar{x}=23.94,\ s=0.13\).

  1. \(H_a:\mu\ne24\);\; \(H_0:\mu=24\).
  2. Two-tailed t, reject if \( t\le -1.984\) or \( t\ge 1.984\) (use \(df=99\) or nearest).
  3. \(\displaystyle t=\frac{23.94-24}{0.13/\sqrt{100}}=-4.615\) → Reject \(H_0\).
  4. 95% CI: \(E=t_{.025,\,df}\,(s/\sqrt{n})=1.984\cdot(0.13/10)=0.026\). CI \(=23.94\pm0.026=(23.914,\ 23.966)\).
  5. Consistency: since 24 is outside the CI, the two-tailed test at \( \alpha=0.05\) agrees.

Example — Metal Components (Right-Tailed t)

Claim: population mean \(>\,4.5\) oz. Use \( \alpha=0.05\). Given \( n=10,\ \bar{x}=4.59,\ s=0.504\).

  1. \(H_a:\mu>4.5\);\; \(H_0:\mu\le4.5\).
  2. Right-tailed t; reject if \( t\ge1.833\) (with \(df=9\)).
  3. \(\displaystyle t=\frac{4.59-4.5}{0.504/\sqrt{10}}=0.565\).
  4. \(0.565<1.833\) → Fail to Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Insufficient evidence that the mean \(>\,4.5\) oz.

Example — Lexington Household Income (Left-Tailed t)

Claim: mean income \(<\$50{,}000\). Use \( \alpha=0.05\). Given \( n=100,\ \bar{x}=\$40{,}571,\ s=\$8{,}316\).

  1. \(H_a:\mu<50{,}000\);\; \(H_0:\mu\ge50{,}000\).
  2. Left-tailed t; reject if \( t\le -1.661\) (with \(df=99\)).
  3. \(\displaystyle t=\frac{40571-50000}{8316/\sqrt{100}}=-11.338\).
  4. \(-11.338\le -1.661\) → Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Conclude (95% confidence) the mean is less than \$50,000.

Example — Pig Weight Change (Two-Tailed t)

Claim: average weight change differs from 200 lb. Use \( \alpha=0.05\). Given \( n=24,\ \bar{x}=197.2,\ s=9.8\).

  1. \(H_a:\mu\ne200\);\; \(H_0:\mu=200\).
  2. Two-tailed t; reject if \( t\le -2.069\) or \( t\ge 2.069\) (with \(df=23\)).
  3. \(\displaystyle t=\frac{197.2-200}{9.8/\sqrt{24}}=-1.400\).
  4. \(-2.069< -1.400 < 2.069\) → Fail to Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Insufficient evidence of a change from 200 lb.

Test of Hypothesis for a Proportion \( p \) (Large \( n \))

For sufficiently large \( n \), use a Z test with the null proportion \( p \) and \( q=1-p \): \[ Z \;=\; \frac{\hat{p}-p}{\sqrt{pq/n}}. \]

Examples — Proportions

Cancer Remission (Right-Tailed)

Current method \(p=0.20\). New method: 26 of 100 remissions (\(\hat p=0.26\)). \( \alpha=0.05\).

  1. \(H_a:p>0.20\);\; \(H_0:p\le0.20\).
  2. Reject if \( Z\ge1.645\).
  3. \(\displaystyle Z=\frac{0.26-0.20}{\sqrt{(0.20)(0.80)/100}}=1.5\).
  4. \(1.5<1.645\) → Fail to Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Insufficient evidence the new method is better.

Vanilla Wafers (Left-Tailed)

Question: Is \(p<0.50\)? Sample: 42 of 250 choose Murray’s (\(\hat p=0.168\)). \( \alpha=0.05\).

  1. \(H_a:p<0.50\);\; \(H_0:p\ge0.50\).
  2. Reject if \( Z\le -1.645\).
  3. \(\displaystyle Z=\frac{0.168-0.50}{\sqrt{(0.50)(0.50)/250}}=-10.499\).
  4. \(-10.499\le -1.645\) → Reject \(H_0\).
  5. Conclude (95% confidence) less than 50% prefer Murray’s.
