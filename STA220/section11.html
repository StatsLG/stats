Section 11 — Inferences Based on Two Samples

We compare two population means \( \mu_1 \) and \( \mu_2 \). The correct method depends on the design: dependent (paired) samples or independent samples. For each design we cover hypotheses, test statistics, critical regions, and confidence intervals.

Design: Dependent vs Independent Samples

Independent samples: values in one sample are unrelated to those in the other (e.g., randomly assign different subjects to two teaching methods; randomized drug vs placebo arms).

Dependent (paired) samples: subjects are intentionally paired to be alike, or the same subject receives both treatments. Pairing reduces subject-to-subject variability, improving precision when that variability is large relative to expected treatment differences.

Dependent (Paired) Samples — Notation & Setup

Let pair \(j\) have measurements \(x_{1j}\) (treatment 1) and \(x_{2j}\) (treatment 2). Define the difference

\[ d_j = x_{1j} - x_{2j}. \]

We analyze the set of differences \(d_1,\dots,d_n\). The difference mean and variance estimate

  • \(\mu_d=\mu_1-\mu_2\) (parameter of interest),
  • point estimate \( \bar d=\bar x_1-\bar x_2 \),
  • sample SD \( s_d \).

Sample Variance\(s_1^2\)\(s_2^2\)\(s_d^2\)
Sample SD\(s_1\)\(s_2\)\(s_d\)

Interpreting \( \mu_d \)

  • If \( \mu_d>0 \Rightarrow \mu_1>\mu_2 \)
  • If \( \mu_d<0 \Rightarrow \mu_1<\mu_2 \)
  • If \( \mu_d\ne 0 \Rightarrow \mu_1\ne \mu_2 \)

Dependent Samples — t Test & Confidence Interval

Test statistic (df \(=n-1\)):

\[ t \;=\; \frac{\bar d - \mu_d}{\,s_d/\sqrt{n}\,}. \]

Two-sided \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) CI for \( \mu_d \):

\[ \bar d \;\pm\; t_{\alpha/2,\;n-1}\,\frac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}}. \]

Hypotheses & Critical Region

\(H_a:\mu_d>0\) (equivalently \( \mu_1>\mu_2 \)), \(H_0:\mu_d\le 0\).

Right-tailed t test, \(df=5\): reject if \(t\ge 2.015\).

Test Statistic

\[ t=\frac{2-0}{1.6733/\sqrt{6}}=2.928. \]

\(2.928\ge 2.015\) → Reject \(H_0\).

Conclude with 95% confidence that the new display increases sales.

Paired 95% Confidence Interval — Worked

Margin \( E=t_{\alpha/2,5}\dfrac{s_d}{\sqrt{n}} = 2.571\cdot\dfrac{1.6733}{\sqrt{6}}=1.756.\)

\[ \bar d \pm E \;=\; 2\pm1.756 \;=\; (0.244,\;3.756). \]

Interpretation: we are 95% confident the mean increase is between 0.244 and 3.756 cases.

Independent Samples — Notation & Concept

Treatment 1Treatment 2
Sample Size\(n_1\)\(n_2\)
Data\(x_{11},x_{12},\dots,x_{1n_1}\)\(x_{21},x_{22},\dots,x_{2n_2}\)
Sample Mean\(\bar x_1\)\(\bar x_2\)
Sample Variance\(s_1^2\)\(s_2^2\)
Sample SD\(s_1\)\(s_2\)

The point estimate of \( \mu_1-\mu_2 \) is \( \bar x_1-\bar x_2 \) with standard error \( \sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1+\sigma_2^2/n_2} \). For large \(n_1,n_2\), the standardized statistic

\[ Z=\frac{(\bar x_1-\bar x_2)-(\mu_1-\mu_2)}{\sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1+\sigma_2^2/n_2}} \]

is approximately standard normal (in practice, population variances are unknown, see below).

Independent Samples (Equal Variances) — Pooled t Test & CI

Assume \( \sigma_1^2=\sigma_2^2 \). Use the pooled variance

\[ s_p^2=\frac{(n_1-1)s_1^2+(n_2-1)s_2^2}{n_1+n_2-2}, \]

then the test statistic (df \(=n_1+n_2-2\)) is

\[ t=\frac{(\bar x_1-\bar x_2)-(\mu_1-\mu_2)}{\sqrt{s_p^2/n_1+s_p^2/n_2}}. \]

A two-sided \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) CI is

\[ (\bar x_1-\bar x_2)\ \pm\ t_{\alpha/2,\;n_1+n_2-2}\ \sqrt{s_p^2/n_1+s_p^2/n_2}. \]

Example — Fuel Additive (Independent)

Compare MPG with additive (T1) vs without (T2). \(n_1=n_2=5\). Use \( \alpha=0.05\) to test if the additive increases mileage.

With Additive (T1)Without Additive (T2)
Data26.3, 27.4, 25.1, 26.8, 27.124.5, 25.4, 23.7, 25.9, 25.7
Means\(\bar x_1=26.54\)\(\bar x_2=25.04\)
Variances\(s_1^2=0.813\)\(s_2^2=0.848\)

Hypotheses & Critical Region

\(H_a:\mu_1-\mu_2>0\) (equivalently \( \mu_1>\mu_2 \)), \(H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2\le 0\).

Right-tailed pooled t test, \(df=8\): reject if \(t\ge 1.860\).

Compute \(s_p^2\), Statistic, Decision

\[ s_p^2=\frac{(5-1)(0.813)+(5-1)(0.848)}{5+5-2}=0.8305. \]

\[ t=\frac{(26.54-25.04)-0}{\sqrt{0.8305/5+0.8305/5}}=2.603. \]

\(2.603\ge1.860\) → Reject \(H_0\). Conclude (95% confidence) the additive improves mileage.

Independent 95% Confidence Interval — Worked

Margin \( E=t_{\alpha/2,8}\sqrt{s_p^2/n_1+s_p^2/n_2}=2.306\cdot\sqrt{0.8305/5+0.8305/5}=1.329.\)

\[ (\bar x_1-\bar x_2)\pm E = (26.54-25.04)\pm1.329 \;=\; 1.50\pm1.329 \;=\; (0.171,\;2.829). \]

Interpretation: we are 95% confident the additive increases MPG by between 0.171 and 2.829 miles.

If Variances Are Unequal (Note)

If \( \sigma_1^2\ne\sigma_2^2 \), do not pool. A reasonable statistic is

\[ t=\frac{(\bar x_1-\bar x_2)-(\mu_1-\mu_2)} {\sqrt{s_1^2/n_1+s_2^2/n_2}}, \]

whose exact distribution is complicated for small samples (Welch’s t uses an approximate df formula). For large \(n_1,n_2\), the distribution is well-approximated by normal. In this course, you will assume equal variances for problems unless stated otherwise.

Choosing a Design & Practical Tips

  • When subject-to-subject variability is large relative to expected treatment differences, consider a paired design to reduce error.
  • Always align your critical region (left/right/two-tailed) with the direction in \(H_a\).
  • Report both a test decision and an interpreted CI to convey effect size and uncertainty.
