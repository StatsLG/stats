The Science of Statistics

Statistics is the study of how to collect, organize, analyze, and interpret information.

Warm-ups

  • How do you define statistics?
  • What do you think of when you hear the term statistics?
  • Where do you see statistics used?

Statistics shows up everywhere—sports broadcasts, medical studies, education, business, and policy.

  • Sports: free-throw % (basketball), QB rating, RBIs, assists
  • Medical: drug success/side-effect rates, remission rates, BP changes
  • Education: note-taking & comprehension, reading scores by sex, GPA trends
  • Politics: approval ratings, reelection projections, support for a bill

Fundamental Elements

Population: the full collection of individuals or items of interest.
Census: measurements taken on an entire population.
  • Sample: a subset of the population we measure.
  • Data: the measurements collected.
  • Variable: any characteristic that can vary across individuals.
  • Statistic: a numerical summary of a sample (changes from sample to sample).
  • Parameter: a numerical summary of a population (fixed but generally unknown without a complete census).

Example — Estimating income in Kentucky

  • Population: all Kentucky residents
  • Sample: the selected residents we measure (e.g., 1,000)
  • Variable: income
  • Data: their recorded incomes
  • Parameter: the true mean income of all Kentucky residents
  • Statistic: the sample mean income

Descriptive vs. Inferential

  • Descriptive statistics: summarize data you have (no inference error).
  • Inferential statistics: use sample data to make statements about a population (has sampling error, which we quantify with probability).

Classify each

  1. Mid-season free-throw percentage for a player — Descriptive
  2. Average GPA from the registrar for all current students — Descriptive
  3. President’s approval rating from a sample of 5,000 adults — Inferential
  4. Graph of this year’s sales for all major automakers — Descriptive
  5. % of students in this class who live in Lexington — Descriptive
  6. Estimate of % of all BCTC students who live in Lexington using this class’s data — Inferential

Mathematical Reasoning

  • Inductive reasoning: inferential stats — bottom-up, reasoning from samples.
  • Deductive reasoning (probability): top-down, properties of samples drawn from a known population.

Course flow: Descriptive → Probability (deductive) → Inferential (inductive).

Types of Data

  • Qualitative (categorical): labels, categories.
  • Quantitative (numeric):
    • Discrete: counts (e.g., # of goals).
    • Continuous: measurements (e.g., time, distance).

Identify the type

  1. Gender — Qualitative
  2. Time to run a mile — Quantitative (Continuous)
  3. Number of soccer goals — Quantitative (Discrete)
  4. Religious preference — Qualitative
  5. Distance to work — Quantitative (Continuous)
  6. Number correct on a test — Quantitative (Discrete)
  7. Social security number — Qualitative

Data Collection

Bias is a systematic push in one direction (e.g., sampling that over-represents certain elements). A valid sample should be representative of the population.

Random sample: determined completely by chance; tends to mirror the population on key variables and lets us quantify error.

Example — Sampling Kentucky incomes, redux

Sampling 1,000 males from Lexington is biased; a good random sample mirrors population composition. Larger samples help sample proportions get closer to population values.

Simple random sample (SRS): every sample of size n has an equal chance of selection.

Error in the Statistical Process

Error is the distance between a statistic and the corresponding parameter.

  • Random sampling error: arises because we use a sample; this is the only error we can calculate.
  • Non-sampling error: other errors not due to the sampling mechanism (e.g., measurement issues, nonresponse, processing).

Unless stated otherwise, “error” in this course refers to random sampling error under a well-collected, representative sample.
