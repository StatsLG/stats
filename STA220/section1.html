The Science of Statistics

Statistics — study of how to collect, organize, analyze, and interpret information.

Advantage: gives a process for estimation and decision making under uncertainty without prejudice (bias).

Warm-ups

Statistics are commonly used in most fields of study and are regularly seen in newspapers, on television, and in professional work.

Examples you might see:

Fundamental Elements of Statistics

Population: the collection of individuals or items of interest.
Census: measurements taken from the entire population.

Census notes

  • U.S. Census Bureau attempts a census every 10 years.
  • Challenges include hard-to-reach groups (e.g., homelessness).
  • Censuses are rare in practice due to cost and feasibility.

When a full census isn’t feasible, we use a subset:

  • Sample: subset of the population we measure.
  • Data: the measurements collected.
  • Variable: any characteristic that can take different values across individuals.
  • Statistic: numerical characteristic of a sample (changes from sample to sample).
  • Parameter: numerical characteristic of a population (fixed, but only known with a perfect census).

Branches of Statistical Applications

  • Descriptive Statistics: methods of summarizing a set of data. (No inference error—summarizing the data you actually have.)
  • Inferential Statistics: using a sample to make statements about a population. (There is error; we quantify it using probability.)

Examples — classify as descriptive or inferential

  1. A basketball player’s free-throw percentage up to mid-season
  2. Average GPA for all current students from the records office
  3. President’s approval rating from a sample of 5,000 U.S. residents
  4. Graph of this year’s sales for all major vehicle manufacturers
  5. % of students in this class who live in Lexington
  6. Estimate of % of all BCTC students who live in Lexington using this class’s data
Answers (click to expand)
  1. Descriptive
  2. Descriptive
  3. Inferential
  4. Descriptive
  5. Descriptive
  6. Inferential

Note: #5 and #6 can use the same data but ask different questions. The group of interest determines whether it’s descriptive or inferential.

Types of Mathematical Reasoning

  • Inductive reasoning: inferential statistics; bottom-up reasoning from samples.
  • Deductive reasoning (probability theory): top-down; properties of samples from a known population.

Course order: 1) Descriptive Statistics → 2) Deductive Reasoning (probability) → 3) Inferential Statistics (inductive).

  • Qualitative (categorical): responses placed into categories; outcomes are non-numeric.
  • Quantitative (numeric): values with numerical meaning

Data Collection

Bias: a prejudice in one direction—occurs when the sample selection favors elements with a particular characteristic.

For inferential statistics, your sample must be representative of the population. Proper random sampling minimizes bias and lets us quantify error.

Random sample: determined completely by chance; typically mirrors the population on key variables and allows error control via probability.

Example — Kentucky incomes

Sampling 1,000 males from Lexington would likely overestimate statewide average income (males and Lexington residents tend to earn more). A good random sample reflects population composition (e.g., 50% male if population is 50% male; ≈10% from Lexington if population is ≈10%).

Like coin flips: with larger sample sizes, the sample proportion is more likely to be close to the population value.

Simple Random Sample (SRS)

A simple random sample of size n is selected so that every sample of size n has an equal probability of being chosen.

Classroom example: 5 rows × 10 students each; selecting one entire row of 10 is not an SRS. While each individual may have a 1/5 chance, not every possible group of 10 can be selected (you can only be with your row), so samples do not have equal probability.

Note: In this course we assume data come from an SRS unless stated otherwise.

Statistics as Random Variables

The value of a statistic depends on which individuals are sampled; under random sampling, the statistic itself is a random variable. This is the foundation for the probability calculations we study next.

Error in the Statistical Process

Error: distance between a statistic and the corresponding parameter.

  • Random sampling error: arises when using a random sample to estimate a population. This is the only type of error we can calculate.
  • Non-sampling error: errors not due to the sampling process (e.g., measurement issues, nonresponse, processing).

Unless otherwise noted, when we refer to “error” in this course we mean random sampling error under a good (representative) sample.
