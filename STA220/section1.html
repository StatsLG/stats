The Science of Statistics

Statistics is the study of how to collect, organize, analyze, and interpret information.

Warm‑ups:

  • How do you define statistics?
  • What do you think of when you hear the term statistics?
  • Where do you see statistics used?

Statistics appears across countless domains—news, sports broadcasts, professional work, and academic research. Examples include:

Examples by domain
  • Sports: free‑throw percentage (basketball), QB rating (football), RBIs (baseball), assists (soccer)
  • Medical: success rates and side‑effect likelihood for a drug; remission rate for a cancer; effect of a drug on blood pressure
  • Education: note‑taking and comprehension; reading scores by sex in 1st grade; depression and academic achievement
  • Politics: reelection predictions; senate approval rating; percent supporting a new bill in Kentucky

Key idea: Statistics provides a principled process for estimation and decision‑making under uncertainty and strives to minimize bias.

Fundamental Elements of Statistics

Population: the full collection of individuals or items of interest.

Examples: (1) all U.S. residents; (2) all hospital admits in Lexington, KY.

Census: measurements taken on an entire population.

In practice, a true census is rare and costly. Even the U.S. census every 10 years faces challenges (for instance, reaching people experiencing homelessness) despite legal authority that compels participation.

Because full populations are often infeasible to measure, we study a smaller group instead:

  • Sample: a subset of the population we measure.
  • Data: the measurements collected.
  • Variable: any characteristic that can vary across individuals.
  • Statistic: a numerical summary of a sample (changes from sample to sample).
  • Parameter: a numerical summary of a population (fixed but typically unknown without a perfect census).
Example: estimating income in Kentucky

We sample 1,000 Kentucky residents and record their income.

  • Population: all Kentucky residents.
  • Sample: the 1,000 selected residents.
  • Data: the 1,000 recorded incomes.
  • Variable: income.
  • Parameter: the average income of all Kentucky residents.
  • Statistic: the sample’s average income.
  • If we measured everyone: that would be a census.

Branches of Statistical Applications

  • Descriptive statistics: summarize data you have (no inference error).
  • Inferential statistics: use sample data to make statements about a population (has sampling error; we quantify and control this with probability).
Classify each as descriptive or inferential
  1. Mid‑season free‑throw percentage for a player
  2. Average GPA using the registrar’s complete records for all current students
  3. President’s approval rating from a sample of 5,000 U.S. residents
  4. Graph of this year’s sales for all major automakers
  5. % of students in this class who live in Lexington
  6. Estimate of % of all BCTC students who live in Lexington using data from this class

Answers: (1) Descriptive, (2) Descriptive, (3) Inferential, (4) Descriptive, (5) Descriptive, (6) Inferential.

Types of Mathematical Reasoning

  • Inductive reasoning: inferential statistics — bottom‑up, reasoning by consistency.
  • Deductive reasoning (probability): top‑down, properties of samples from a known population.

Course flow: Descriptive → Probability (deductive) → Inferential (inductive).

Types of Data

  • Qualitative (categorical): non‑numeric categories.
  • Quantitative (numeric): values with numeric meaning.
    • Discrete: counts.
    • Continuous: measurements.
Identify the data type
  1. Gender — Qualitative
  2. Time to run a mile — Quantitative (Continuous)
  3. Number of soccer goals — Quantitative (Discrete)
  4. Religious preference — Qualitative
  5. Distance to work — Quantitative (Continuous)
  6. Number correct on a test — Quantitative (Discrete)
  7. Social security number — Qualitative

Data Collection

Bias is a systematic prejudice in one direction, often due to sampling that favors certain elements.

To make valid inferences, a sample must be representative of the population. Using proper sampling techniques helps minimize bias.

Random sample: determined completely by chance; tends to mirror the population on key variables and lets us quantify error.

Example: sampling Kentucky incomes, redux

Sampling 1,000 males from Lexington is biased (males and Lexington residents tend to earn more). A good random sample aligns the sample’s composition with the population’s.

Increasing the sample size improves the chance your sample proportions are close to population values (similar to more coin flips getting you closer to 50% heads).

Simple random sample (SRS): each sample of size n has an equal chance of being selected.
Not every “equal person chance” is SRS

Selecting one classroom row of 10 students from 5 rows gives each person a 1/5 chance, but not every possible set of 10 students is equally possible—so it’s not an SRS.

Error in the Statistical Process

Error is the distance between a statistic and the corresponding parameter.

  • Random sampling error: arises because we use a sample; this is the only error we can calculate.
  • Non‑sampling error: all other errors not due to the sampling mechanism.

In this course, unless otherwise stated, “error” refers to random sampling error under the assumption of a well‑collected, representative sample.
