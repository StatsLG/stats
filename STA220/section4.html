Counting Rules

Examples

  • Arrange SUNDAY (6 distinct letters): \(6\cdot5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot2\cdot1 = 720\).
  • 3-digit lock code (digits 0–9, repetition allowed): \(10\cdot10\cdot10 = 10^3 = 1000\).

Factorials

\(n!\) is the product of integers from \(n\) down to 1. By convention, \(0!=1\).

\(1!=1\)

\(2!=2\cdot1=2\)

\(3!=3\cdot2\cdot1=6\)

\(4!=24\)

Simplifying with cancellations

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{5!}{2!}=\frac{5\cdot4\cdot3\cdot\cancel{2!}}{\cancel{2!}}=60\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{9!}{8!}=\frac{9\cdot\cancel{8!}}{\cancel{8!}}=9\)
  • \(\displaystyle \frac{8!}{2!\,6!}=\frac{8\cdot7\cdot\cancel{6!}}{2\cdot1\cdot\cancel{6!}}=\frac{56}{2}=28\)

Permutations (order matters)

Number of ordered arrangements of \(r\) objects from \(n\): \(\displaystyle \,^{n}P_r=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\).

Example

Select a president, vice president, treasurer, and secretary from 10 people (order matters): \(\;^{10}P_4 = 10\cdot9\cdot8\cdot7 = 5040\).

Combinations (order does not matter)

Number of unordered selections of \(r\) from \(n\): \(\displaystyle \,^{n}C_r=\frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\).

Example

Committee of 5 senators from 8: \(\;^{8}C_5=\frac{8!}{5!\,3!}=56\).

Sample Spaces & Events

  • Coin flip: \(S=\{H,T\}\) (qualitative outcome).
  • Roll a die: \(S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\) (discrete quantitative).
  • Measure heights: \(S=[\text{shortest},\text{tallest}]\) (continuous; uncountably many outcomes).

Event = subset of the sample space (e.g., even on a die: \(\{2,4,6\}\)).

Probability of event \(A\): long-run proportion \(P(A)=\dfrac{\#\text{ outcomes in }A}{\#\text{ outcomes in }S}\) (for equally likely outcomes).

Tree Diagram: Penny, Nickel, Dime

Each coin has two outcomes ⇒ \(2\cdot2\cdot2=8\) outcomes total.

Sample Space

\(\{HHH,\ HHT,\ HTH,\ HTT,\ THH,\ THT,\ TTH,\ TTT\}\)

  • \(A=\{\text{all tails}\}=\{TTT\}\Rightarrow P(A)=1/8\)
  • \(B=\{\text{exactly two tails}\}=\{HTT,THT,TTH\}\Rightarrow P(B)=3/8\)
  • \(C=\{\text{exactly one tail}\}=\{HHT,HTH,THH\}\Rightarrow P(C)=3/8\)
  • \(D=\{\text{no tails}\}=\{HHH\}\Rightarrow P(D)=1/8\)

Probability Properties

  • \(0\le P(A)\le1\)
  • \(P(S)=1\)
  • Higher probability ⇒ more likely

Types of Probability

  • Simple: one event.
  • Joint: uses intersection \(A\cap B\) or union \(A\cup B\).
  • Mutually exclusive (disjoint): \(P(A\cap B)=0\).

Addition & Complement Rules

  • Addition: \(P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)\).
  • Complement: \(P(A^c)=1-P(A)\).

Conditional Probability

\(P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}\). The condition changes the denominator to the size/probability of \(B\).

Simple probabilities

\(P(A)=6/36=0.167\)

\(P(B)=16/36=0.444\)

\(P(C)=6/36=0.167\)

Intersections

\(P(A\cap B)=2/36=0.056\) (pairs (3,4),(4,3))

\(P(A\cap C)=1/36=0.028\)

\(P(B\cap C)=4/36=0.111\)

Unions

\(P(A\cup B)=6/36+16/36-2/36=20/36=0.556\)

\(P(A\cup C)=6/36+6/36-1/36=11/36=0.306\)

\(P(B\cup C)=16/36+6/36-4/36=18/36=0.5\)

Complements

\(P(A^c)=1-6/36=30/36=0.833\)

\(P(B^c)=1-16/36=20/36=0.556\)

\(P(C^c)=1-6/36=30/36=0.833\)

Conditionals (order matters)

\(P(A\mid B)=\dfrac{2/36}{16/36}=2/16=0.125\)

\(P(B\mid A)=\dfrac{2/36}{6/36}=2/6=0.333\)

\(P(A\mid C)=\dfrac{1/36}{6/36}=1/6=0.167\)

\(P(C\mid A)=\dfrac{1/36}{6/36}=1/6=0.167\)

\(P(B\mid C)=\dfrac{4/36}{6/36}=4/6=0.667\)

\(P(C\mid B)=\dfrac{4/36}{16/36}=4/16=0.25\)

Independence vs Dependence

Events \(A,B\) are independent if \(P(A\mid B)=P(A)\); otherwise dependent.

Multiplication Rule

\(P(A\cap B)=P(A)\,P(B\mid A)\). Derived by rearranging the conditional definition.

Example — Two Aces from a 52-card Deck

Without replacement

Dependent: if first is an ace, second’s probability changes to \(3/51\).

\(P(\text{both aces})=(4/52)\cdot(3/51)\approx0.004525\).

With replacement

Independent: shuffling restores \(P(\text{ace})=4/52\) on the second draw.

\(P(\text{both aces})=(4/52)\cdot(4/52)\approx0.00591\).
